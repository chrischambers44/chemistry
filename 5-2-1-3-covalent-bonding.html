Core idea for 5.2.1.3

Covalent bonding happens when non-metal atoms share pairs of electrons. The shared electrons attract both nuclei, holding the atoms together as a molecule.

How covalent bonds form

  • Between non-metals only
  • Atoms share electrons to get a full outer shell
  • A bond is a shared pair of electrons
  • The result is a molecule (e.g., H2O, CO2)
Key phrase: a covalent bond is the electrostatic attraction between the shared pair of electrons and the nuclei of the bonded atoms.

Diatomic molecules (useful reminder)

  • Some elements exist naturally as diatomic molecules (pairs of atoms)
  • Common examples you must write correctly: H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂

Exam tip: what “explain covalent bonding” usually wants
AQA usually wants:
1) non-metals,
2) share electrons,
3) a shared pair is a covalent bond,
4) atoms share to get a full outer shell.

Common mark-scheme phrases (copy-friendly)

Use these as building blocks for 2–4 mark explanations.

Core bonding phrases

“Covalent bonding occurs between non-metal atoms.”
“The atoms share pairs of electrons.”

Full outer shell

“Each atom shares electrons to achieve a full outer shell.”
“A covalent bond is a shared pair of electrons.”

Exam clarity

“The shared electrons are attracted to the nuclei of both atoms.”
“This electrostatic attraction holds the atoms together as a molecule.”

Diatomic reminder

“Some elements exist as diatomic molecules (e.g., Cl₂ and O₂).”
Tip: If you say “shared pair of electrons” and “full outer shell”, you usually secure key marks.
