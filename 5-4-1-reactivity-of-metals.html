AQA 5.4.1 Tutorial – Reactivity of metals

This lesson covers how the reactivity series predicts reactions (including displacement), and how it links to extraction and oxidation/reduction.

Core idea for 5.4.1

Metal reactivity is about how easily a metal forms positive ions by losing electrons. A more reactive metal loses electrons more easily, so it reacts more readily.

5.4.1.1 Metals and their compounds

  • Metals form positive ions (cations) by losing electrons.
  • Example: Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻
  • Metal compounds contain metal ions (e.g. MgO has Mg²⁺ and O²⁻).
  • In reactions, the more reactive metal tends to form ions.
Useful: oxidation = loss of electrons (also “gain of oxygen” in some contexts).

5.4.1.2 The reactivity series

  • Common order (top = most reactive): K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe, Cu, Ag, Au
  • Carbon and hydrogen are included for extraction comparisons.
  • More reactive metals react more vigorously with water and acids.
Memory: “K Na Ca Mg Al (C) Zn Fe (H) Cu Ag Au”

Explanation

Displacement reactions (using the series)

  • A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its compound.
  • Example: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu
  • If the added metal is less reactive → no reaction.

Extraction & redox (links to 5.4.3)

  • Metals occur as ores (compounds).
  • Metals below carbon can often be extracted by reduction with carbon.
  • Metals above carbon must be extracted by electrolysis.
  • Reduction (GCSE): loss of oxygen. (HT also: gain of electrons.)
Quick check (extraction)

Explanation
Key vocabulary
Reactivity series – list of metals in order of reactivity.
Displacement – a more reactive metal replaces a less reactive one in a compound.
Oxidation – loss of electrons (or gain of oxygen).
Reduction – gain of electrons (or loss of oxygen).
Ore – rock containing enough metal compound to extract profitably.
