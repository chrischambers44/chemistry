Core idea for 5.2.1.2

Ionic bonding happens when a metal transfers electrons to a non-metal. This forms ions. The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions is the ionic bond.

How ions form

  • Metals lose electrons → positive ions (cations)
  • Non-metals gain electrons → negative ions (anions)
  • The number of electrons transferred links to the ion charge
Examples:
Na → Na⁺ + e⁻ (Group 1 metal)
Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ (Group 2 metal)
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (Group 7 non-metal)

How formulas work (GCSE-level)

  • Ionic compounds have overall charge = 0
  • So the charges must “balance”
  • Example: Ca²⁺ needs two Cl⁻ → formula is CaCl₂
Charge balancing quick method:
Ca2+ and Cl- → “cross over” numbers → CaCl₂

Exam tip: what “explain what happens” usually wants
AQA usually wants:
1) who loses/gains electrons,
2) which ions form (with charges),
3) that opposite charges attract (ionic bond),
4) (sometimes) why the formula has that ratio (charge balance).

Common mark-scheme phrases (copy-friendly)

Use these as building blocks for 2–4 mark explanations.

Electron transfer

“Electrons are transferred from the metal to the non-metal.”
“The metal loses electrons to form a positive ion (cation).”

Ion formation

“The non-metal gains electrons to form a negative ion (anion).”
“Oppositely charged ions attract. This electrostatic attraction is the ionic bond.”

Formulas

“The formula shows the ratio of ions needed to make the overall charge zero.”
“Two chloride ions are needed to balance one calcium ion (Ca²⁺), so the formula is CaCl₂.”

Link to the chlorine question

“Calcium loses two electrons; each chlorine atom gains one electron, forming Ca²⁺ and 2Cl⁻.”
Tip: Write charges clearly. If you include ion charges, you usually pick up marks quickly.
