Core ideas for 5.1.1.2

A mixture contains two or more substances not chemically bonded. Because they are not bonded, mixtures can be separated by physical methods. The best method depends on properties like solubility and boiling point.

Key definitions

  • Solute: substance that dissolves
  • Solvent: liquid that does the dissolving
  • Solution: solute + solvent (a mixture)
  • Soluble / insoluble: whether it dissolves in a solvent
  • Pure substance: only one substance (not a mixture)
  • Formulation: a mixture designed to have specific properties
Example formulation: paint (pigment + solvent + binder + additives).

How to choose a separation method

Mixture type Property used Method
Insoluble solid + liquid Particle size / insoluble Filtration
Soluble solid dissolved in liquid Solubility changes / remove solvent Crystallisation (or evaporation)
Liquid + liquid (different b.p.) Boiling point difference Simple distillation
Several liquids (different b.p., close together) Boiling points + fractionating column Fractional distillation
Dyes/inks in a solution Different solubilities / attractions Chromatography

Filtration

Use filtration to separate an insoluble solid from a liquid.
  • Solid stays as residue on filter paper
  • Liquid that passes through is filtrate

Crystallisation (getting a soluble solid back)

Used to obtain a soluble solid from a solution.
  • Heat solution to evaporate some solvent (make it concentrated)
  • Leave to cool → crystals form
  • Filter and dry crystals

Simple distillation

Separates a liquid from a solution (or two liquids with very different b.p.).
  • Heat → lowest b.p. liquid boils first
  • Vapour condenses → collected as distillate

Fractional distillation

Separates mixtures of liquids with different boiling points.
  • Uses a fractionating column
  • Different liquids condense at different heights
  • Liquids are collected as separate fractions

Chromatography

Separates substances (like dyes) dissolved in a solvent.
  • Mixture travels up paper with solvent
  • More soluble substances move further
  • Some stick more strongly to paper and move less
Required practical link (GCSE)
Chromatography is a required practical: you should be able to describe method, interpret chromatograms, and calculate Rf if needed.

Pure substances & formulations

Pure substances have fixed melting/boiling points.
Mixtures melt/boil over a range of temperatures.

Formulations are mixtures designed to have specific properties.

Common mark-scheme phrases (copy-friendly)

Use these phrases to hit the mark points in 2–6 mark “method/explain” questions.

Mixtures

“A mixture contains two or more substances that are not chemically bonded.”
“Mixtures can be separated by physical methods because no new substance has formed.”

Filtration

“The insoluble solid is the residue and the liquid that passes through is the filtrate.”

Crystallisation

“Evaporate some of the solvent to make the solution concentrated, then cool to form crystals.”

Distillation

“The component with the lower boiling point evaporates first, then condenses to form the distillate.”

Chromatography

“Substances separate because they have different solubilities in the solvent and different attractions to the paper.”

Purity & formulations

“A pure substance has a fixed melting point, but a mixture melts over a range of temperatures.”
“A formulation is a mixture designed as a useful product with specific properties.”
