Core idea for 5.2.1.4

You can predict many simple covalent molecules by using the group number: it tells you how many outer-shell electrons an atom has, and how many more it needs for a full outer shell.

Pattern table (major groups → typical simple molecules)

These are the common GCSE patterns AQA expects you to recognise.
Group Outer e⁻ Typical bonds needed (to fill shell) Common simple molecules / examples
0 (noble gases) Full shell 0 Monatomic atoms (He, Ne, Ar) — do not usually form molecules
7 (halogens) 7 1 Diatomic molecules: F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂
6 6 2 Often forms 2 bonds in molecules: H₂O; elemental oxygen O₂
5 5 3 Often forms 3 bonds: NH₃; elemental nitrogen N₂
4 4 4 Often forms 4 bonds: CH₄ (methane)
Rule of thumb for simple molecules:
Bonds needed = 8 − outer electrons (for these non-metals).

How to use the pattern quickly

  • Find the element’s group number → number of outer electrons
  • Work out how many it needs for 8 → how many bonds it typically forms
  • Use that to predict a simple formula (e.g., N forms 3 bonds → NH₃)
Exam tip: AQA often asks “Explain why” (e.g. why chlorine is Cl₂) or “Draw a dot-and-cross diagram”. Even if you don’t draw, stating the number of outer electrons + needs 1/2/3/4 usually gets marks.

Exam tip: how to answer “Why is chlorine Cl₂?”
Say: chlorine is Group 7, has 7 outer electrons, so each atom needs 1 more for a full shell. Two chlorine atoms share one pair of electrons, forming a single covalent bond, so the molecule is Cl₂.

Common mark-scheme phrases (copy-friendly)

These help with “explain why” and dot-and-cross style questions.

Using group number

“The group number tells you how many electrons are in the outer shell.”
“The atoms share electrons to achieve a full outer shell.”

Explaining patterns

“A covalent bond is a shared pair of electrons.”
“Two atoms can share one pair of electrons so each gets a full outer shell (e.g. Cl₂).”

Simple molecules

“Oxygen (Group 6) typically forms two covalent bonds in simple molecules.”
“Nitrogen (Group 5) typically forms three covalent bonds in simple molecules.”

Noble gases

“Noble gases already have a full outer shell, so they are unreactive and exist as single atoms.”
Tip: For pattern questions, always mention outer electrons + how many needed for a full shell.
