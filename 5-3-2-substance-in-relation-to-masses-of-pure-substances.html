AQA 5.3.2 Tutorial – Use of amount of substance (moles)

Core ideas for 5.3.2

The mole is a way of counting particles. In GCSE calculations, it links: mass ↔ moles ↔ balanced equation ratios.

5.3.2.1 The mole (foundation idea)

  • n = amount of substance in moles
  • m = mass in grams
  • Mr = relative formula mass (from 5.3.1)
Main equation:
n = m ÷ Mr   (and rearrange: m = n × Mr)
Example: How many moles in 36 g of water (Mr=18)?
n = 36 ÷ 18 = 2 mol

5.3.2.2 Reacting masses from equations (core skill)

The coefficients in a balanced equation show the mole ratio. You use them to convert between moles of different substances.
Step What you do Key check
1 Write the balanced equation Coefficients correct
2 Calculate the Mr values you need Use correct Ar
3 Convert given mass → moles (n = m ÷ Mr) Units in grams
4 Use the mole ratio from equation to find moles needed/made Use coefficients as ratios
5 Convert moles → mass (m = n × Mr) Answer in grams
Very common trap: students try to use masses directly from the equation. The equation gives mole ratios, not mass ratios.

Worked example (like the ammonia question)

Reaction: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

If you want 34 g of NH₃, what mass of H₂ is needed?

1) Mr(NH₃) = 14 + 3×1 = 17
2) n(NH₃) = 34 ÷ 17 = 2 mol
3) Ratio: 3 mol H₂ makes 2 mol NH₃ → for 2 mol NH₃ you need 3 mol H₂
4) Mr(H₂)=2 → mass = 3 × 2 = 6 g
Notice: the “2” and “3” in the equation are the key ratio.

Exam tip: “limiting reactant” (light-touch)
Sometimes the question says one reactant is in excess. That means the other one is limiting and controls how much product forms. If it says “oxygen is in excess”, don’t calculate oxygen.

Common mark-scheme phrases (copy-friendly)

Use these in calculation explanations and method marks.

Moles + Mr

“Number of moles = mass ÷ relative formula mass (n = m ÷ Mᵣ).”
“Mᵣ is calculated by adding the relative atomic masses in the formula.”

Using the equation ratio

“The coefficients in the balanced equation give the mole ratio.”
“Use the mole ratio to convert from moles of one substance to another.”

Finishing the calculation

“Mass = moles × Mᵣ (m = n × Mᵣ).”

Common warning

“The equation gives mole ratios, not mass ratios.”
