AQA 5.1.3 Tutorial – Properties of transition metals

This module covers 5.1.3 (AQA Trilogy Chemistry): what makes transition metals different from Group 1 metals, and the key GCSE properties you must learn: higher density, higher melting points, less reactive, plus their special chemistry: coloured compounds and use as catalysts.

Core ideas for 5.1.3

Transition metals are the block of metals in the middle of the periodic table. They are typical metals (conduct, strong), but compared with Group 1 they are usually less reactive, have higher densities and higher melting points. Many also form coloured compounds and can act as catalysts.

What counts as a transition metal (GCSE level)

  • Found in the central block of the periodic table
  • Examples you might see: iron, copper, nickel, chromium
  • Many are used because they are strong and good conductors
The “transition metals” in GCSE are usually described as the metals between Groups 2 and 3 in the centre block.

How they compare with Group 1 metals

Property Group 1 metals Transition metals
Reactivity Very reactive Less reactive
Density Low (some float) Higher density
Melting point Relatively low Higher melting point
Compounds Usually white/colourless salts Often form coloured compounds
Catalysts Not common in GCSE examples Many are good catalysts
If asked “compare”, use the words: less reactive, higher melting point, higher density.

Coloured compounds (why it matters)

  • Many transition metal ions/compounds are coloured
  • Colour can help identify ions in tests (you may meet this later)
  • Examples you might see at GCSE:
    • Copper compounds often blue/green
    • Iron compounds can be green/brown (varies)
Exam-safe phrasing: “Transition metals often form coloured compounds.” (You don’t usually need to memorise exact colours unless the question gives them.)

Catalysts (big GCSE idea)

  • A catalyst speeds up a reaction
  • It is not used up (chemically unchanged at the end)
  • Transition metals and their compounds are often used as catalysts
  • Common named example: iron is used as a catalyst in the Haber process
“Not used up” is a very common mark point.

Exam tip: avoid over-claiming about colours
It’s safe to say “transition metal compounds are often coloured”. Don’t guess specific colours unless the question provides a clue or it’s a standard example (e.g. copper compounds often blue/green).

Common mark-scheme phrases (copy-friendly)

Use these for “compare/explain” questions on transition metals.

Comparison with Group 1

“Transition metals are less reactive than Group 1 metals.”
“Transition metals have higher melting points and higher densities than Group 1 metals.”

Coloured compounds

“Transition metal compounds are often coloured.”

Catalysts

“A catalyst increases the rate of a reaction and is not used up.”
“Iron is used as a catalyst in the Haber process.”

Structure/location

“Transition metals are found in the central block of the periodic table.”
Tip: For a 3–4 marker, aim for 3 separate points: reactivity, density/melting point, and coloured compounds or catalysts.
