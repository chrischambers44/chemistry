Core ideas for 5.7.1

Crude oil is a finite resource and is a mixture of many hydrocarbons. We separate crude oil into fractions using fractional distillation. As molecules get bigger, boiling point and viscosity increase, but flammability decreases.

5.7.1.1 Crude oil

  • Finite resource (used faster than it forms)
  • Formed from ancient biomass (mainly plankton) buried in mud
  • A mixture of a very large number of compounds
  • Most are hydrocarbons (made of carbon and hydrogen only)
Exam wording: “Crude oil is a mixture of hydrocarbons.”

Hydrocarbons & alkanes

  • Hydrocarbon = compound containing carbon and hydrogen only
  • Alkanes are a family (homologous series) of hydrocarbons
  • General formula: CnH2n+2
  • First four alkanes: methane, ethane, propane, butane

5.7.1.2 Fractional distillation

Separates crude oil because different hydrocarbons have different boiling points.
  • Crude oil is heated → vapours rise up a column
  • Column is hot at the bottom, cooler at the top
  • Each fraction condenses at a different height
  • Fractions contain molecules with a similar number of carbon atoms
Typical fractions (examples)Main uses
LPG, petrol, kerosene, diesel, heavy fuel oilFuels for heating/transport

Petrochemicals (feedstock)

Fractions can be processed to make useful materials (feedstock for the petrochemical industry), e.g.:
  • Solvents
  • Lubricants
  • Polymers
  • Detergents
“Feedstock” = raw material used to make other chemicals/products.

5.7.1.3 Properties of hydrocarbons (trend with size)

As hydrocarbon molecules get bigger…TrendWhy it matters
Boiling pointincreasesHarder to vaporise; condense lower in the column
ViscosityincreasesThicker/less runny (e.g. oils)
FlammabilitydecreasesHarder to ignite (less volatile)

Complete combustion of hydrocarbons

Complete combustion (plenty of oxygen):
hydrocarbon + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water
Example (propane):
C3H8 + 5O2 → 3CO2 + 4H2O
Quick method to balance combustion equations
1) Balance C → make CO2 match carbon atoms.
2) Balance H → make H2O match hydrogen atoms (divide by 2).
3) Count O on the right → balance O2 on the left.
If you get a half, multiply everything by 2.
